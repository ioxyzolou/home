Project 3: Building an Alarm Clock

Posted on October 12, 2023 by Ifigenia Oxyzolou

This week, I built an alarm clock by soldering a circuit board and designed and 3D-printed a casing for it!

Soldering

The first necessary step in building our alarm clock was soldering each of the components to the electronic board. This process was a bit difficult to get the hang of at first. At first, the solder was messy and I had to do a lot of cleaning and redoing. Eventually though I was able to solder pretty quickly and without spreading the solder everywhere.

One big challenge was correcting the alignment of some of our pieces. The time display pieces were placed very messily at first and so I had to re-do some of my work by vacuuming the solder. I found this to be a very tedious task and struggled to remove all the solder. When I had removed all the solder and tried to pull the time display piece out to realign it, I uprooted a part of the board, breaking a connection. In order to fix this issue, I had to use a piece of solder to replace and wire that connection. This was a valuable learning experience.

Once we finished all the soldering for the clock, we connected it to a battery and it started working successfully!

Designing the Casing

In order to design the casing for our alarm clock, we had to take precise measurements of its dimensions. Pictured below is the detailed sketch with all the dimensions for our alarm clock.

I took all these measurements in order to create a precise frame for the clock on Autodesk Fusion 360. For the casing design I created on Autodesk Fusion 360, I increased these measurements by approximately 1 mm in order to make them nicely hug the board's perimeter as well as the holes for the time display and the buttons. I additionally created holes for the speaker to be amplified through. When thinking about how I would create a design that could hold both the electronic board of the clock and hide the wiring and battery, I had the idea to make a two-piece casing. The top piece of the case would hold the circuit board, and this piece would seamlessly slip into a larger back piece. The initial idea for this can be seen in my very rough sketch below.

3D Printing the Casing

My partner, Gil, and I designed the casing for this clock in Autodesk Fusion 360. The biggest challenge I found was ensuring the dimensions for the top piece of the alarm clock aligned with the bottom piece. I rounded the edges with the fillet tool and found that this feature cut down the dimensions of my design, so I had to go back to my sketch and increase the dimensions so that when filleted, my design would match my desired dimensions for the clock.

For this print I used relatively default slicer settings. The only change I made was adjusting the layer thickness to 0.20 mm in hopes of finishing our print within class time, leaving time for more drafts. The print time for the lid was 3 hrs 42 min, and around 6 hours for the larger back piece. Unfortunately, we were not able to complete more than one draft of our final design.

Final Product

Overall, I was pretty happy with our final product, as the two pieces of our casing fit together nicely and also fit the clock’s dimensions very well. If I had more time, I would have added some fun features to the clock casing, as well as altering the button feature, since in our final design, we had to use a pen to press the buttons. Regardless, this project was a very fulfilling experience because, despite the design challenges I encountered, I was able to create a usable, tangible product!
