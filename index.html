Building my first Prusa MINI+

Posted on September 8, 2023 by Ifigenia Oxyzolou

This past week in ENGR 11A, I built and calibrated a Prusa MINI+ 3D Printer with my classmate Gianna. Assembling the printer was a fairly straightforward process that I found to be very enjoyable! The instructions were a bit overly detailed which became confusing at times, but the process went smoothly overall. If I could change something, I would simplify the manual and add larger diagrams (kind of like IKEA manuals) as opposed to the small, busy images that were included. I find that style easier to follow. The overall process of putting the printer together was quite easy though.

One challenge was getting the screws all the way through to connect the main base with the part with the nozzle. Additionally, organizing all the wiring of the machine, while not that difficult, was confusing because of the overly detailed instructions. But we managed to get it all to fit nicely into the electronics box as seen below!

When inserting the PLA filament, it took multiple tries to get it to load all the way through the PTFE tube up to the nozzle. The first few inches of the filament were lumpy which was blocking it from getting all the way through. However, we were finally able to get it in after cutting off that piece of the filament.

Calibration

Once we had finished building our printer, calibrating it took a few adjustments in order to get the nozzle to align with the magnetic plate. Our first nozzle test resulted in a sparse square of PLA filament (as pictured below). After a few adjustments though, the nozzle hovered just above the plate and printed a nice even square with clean corners. And with that, Pruscilla was ready!

Benchy!

The first design we printed with Pruscilla was the classic Benchy design with the intent of refining the calibration of our printer. Our first Benchy print had a length of 59.67 mm, a height of 48.16 mm, and a width of 30.77 mm. These dimensions were very close to what the dimensions of a Benchy 3D print should be, (length: 60 mm, height: 48 mm, width: 31 mm). Thus, we knew our printer was already well-calibrated. Our goal with our second print was to play around with the infill and layer height settings to change the speed of the print. Reducing the infill from 15% to 10% greatly improved the speed of our print, allowing it to finish in less than an hour. Increasing the layer height from 0.2 mm to 0.39 mm also made the print go faster, while making the silhouette of the boat more boxy (as can be seen on the top of the Benchy on the right in the picture below).

(L: 1st Benchy print, R: 2nd Benchy print)

I then did a print with a star infill (for fun), and reduced the layer height to 0.1 mm. This print took over 2 hours, but the final Benchy product was silky smooth and had very accurate dimensions (59.9 mm x 48.2 mm x 30.7 mm). I would say this final Benchy was our best one as it was very structurally intact and felt more like it was molded rather than 3D printed.

Captain cat on our third Benchy print!

Ultimately, playing around with the different settings on the Benchy allowed me to better understand how printer speed influences structural integrity and vice versa. Depending on the print, it can be more worthwhile to give more attention to one element over another. Ideally though, efficient prints with good structural integrity might just be the happiest medium.
